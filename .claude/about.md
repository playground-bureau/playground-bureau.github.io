# Playground Bureau & Monkey Business — Project Overview

## What This Is

This document provides context on a real community project and its fictional narrative layer, so that an AI assistant can help with brainstorming, writing, planning, and creative development.

## The Real-World Project

### The Organizer

Luca is a Barcelona-based freelance software engineer (Ruby on Rails, Go) originally from Japan, who has lived in Barcelona for about a decade. Outside of tech work, he runs a community-building project called Playground Bureau (PGB), motivated by a deep concern about social atomization — the trend of people becoming increasingly isolated, disconnected, and lonely in modern life. His thinking draws on Robert Putnam (Bowling Alone), Ray Oldenburg (third places), Eric Klinenberg, Vivek Murthy's loneliness research, and Mark Fisher's work on capitalist realism — the idea that late capitalism forecloses alternative ways of living and relating.

PGB's practical community work includes a weekly community dinner in Barcelona and various events and gatherings designed to create non-commodified spaces for genuine human connection.

### The Organization: APES

The legal entity behind PGB is a registered Spanish cultural association called APES — Associació per a la Promoció dels Enllaços Socials (Association for the Promotion of Social Bonds). It's a non-profit. Its mission statement:

> We are a non-profit association dedicated to co-creating meaning and cultivating belonging in a world increasingly marked by alienation and atomisation. Our vision is to develop and share effective models for human connection that counter alienation and atomisation, which we recognize as closely linked to trends of excessive profit-seeking, hyperindividualism, and over-digitalization of communication. We operate as a living laboratory for community practices, testing and refining approaches that create meaningful face-to-face interactions across diverse groups, celebrate and preserve irreplaceable human creativity and expression, build sustainable structures for mutual support and shared purpose, and bridge digital and physical experiences in ways that enhance rather than replace human contact.

### The Festival: Monkey Business

Monkey Business is PGB's flagship event — a grassroots, non-profit festival held annually near Barcelona (typically late September, three nights/four days). Key facts:

- **Gift economy model**: The ticket price covers everything — food (cooked by volunteer professional chefs), music, workshops, bar (donation-based), camping, all activities. There is zero monetary exchange inside the festival. Nobody gets paid — not organizers, not DJs, not chefs, not workshop leaders. Everyone contributes for the love of it.
- **Scale**: MB1 had ~182 attendees, MB2 had ~240, and MB3 is targeting ~300 capacity.
- **DIY ethos**: The organizing team (~20 coordinator functions) pours months of volunteer effort into producing a 76-hour experience. It's entirely self-funded through ticket sales with no sponsors (in real life — though sponsorship is satirized in the narrative).
- **Tone**: Silly, humor-filled, full of skits and weird names and absurd theatrics — but with a sincere wholesomeness at its core. Participants leave with a warm heart. It's not ironic detachment; the comedy is a vehicle for genuine connection.
- **Music**: DJs (CDJs and Technics 1200s available) and live musicians perform on a gift basis. The festival runs multiple music areas across the weekend.
- **What it's not**: It's not a commercial festival. It's not Burning Man (though it shares some gift-economy DNA). It's a small, intimate, food-and-music-heavy gathering with a strong narrative/theatrical wrapper.

### The Organizing Structure

PGB has scaled to roughly 20 coordinator functions covering areas like music programming, food/kitchen, workshops, logistics, decoration/art, communications, narrative/theatrical production, and more. Luca has been thinking carefully about governance, succession planning, democratic decision-making, and avoiding the project becoming dependent on any single person.

## The Fictional Narrative Layer

The festival is wrapped in an elaborate satirical fictional universe that plays out through skits, theatrical moments, set design, printed materials, and the overall framing of the event. The humor is absurdist and bureaucratic — think Parks and Recreation meets a Cold War-era government agency meets rave culture.

### The Universe

In this parallel timeline, the year is 1980. Catherine "Cat" Sandwich, a previously unknown perennial candidate, defeats Ronald Reagan in a landslide US presidential election with a platform that shifts government focus from GDP to "Goodtimes." This changes everything.

### Key Entities

**PLAYGROUND BUREAU (PGB)** — "Pretends to be good, actually good"
A quasi-government agency elevated from a humble parks office to handle mass production of Goodtimes for the citizenry. Highly organized but occasionally (and hilariously) incompetent. Has a penchant for giving things absurdly long and formal names. The Bureau runs Monkey Business as its flagship annual operation.

**THE SYNDICATE** — "Pretends to be bad, actually good"
A renegade radical activist/revolutionary splinter group, rumored to include disgruntled ex-Bureau agents. They wear tinfoil hats to insulate from "7G." They think the Bureau is too slow and too accommodating of corporate interests. They sneak into Monkey Business to cause trouble — defacing corporate installations, stealing speakers to set up secret parties with edgier vibes. Think Extinction Rebellion vs. the EPA.

**CARREFOOGLE CORPORATION (CFG)** — The corporate antagonist
Represents commercial interests in this universe. In MB2, they sponsored the "scrolling area" (a satirical riff on how Philip Morris sponsors smoking areas). The Syndicate suspects CFG is either contributing to Separatonin or profiting from it. In MB3, the narrative arc centers on Carrefoogle sponsoring the entire festival.

### Key Concepts

**GOODTIMES** — The government's measure of societal success, replacing GDP. What the Bureau is tasked with maximizing.

**GOODTIMESIUM** — The particle (quantum-chemical-neurological, kept intentionally vague) that constitutes Goodtimes. What photons are to light, Goodtimesium is to Goodtimes. The Bureau builds devices to measure and produce it.

**SEPARATONIN** (also referred to as SEPARATOXIN) — The opposing force. A mysterious phenomenon spreading through the population, making people hang out less and spend more time alone. Separatonin and Goodtimesium repel each other. The Bureau's core mission is combating this.

**MONKEY BUSINESS** — In-universe, stands for "Meticulously Orchestrated Neurokinetic Kontrolled Environment for the Yearly Boosting of Universal Sociability, Instilling Neighborly Euphoria, and Spreading Synergy." It's the Bureau's annual operation that gathers willing participants to experiment with new Goodtimesium production methods and mass-produce it so participants can bring it home and spread it "like radioactive glitter."

### Narrative Tone

The tone is a specific blend: absurdist bureaucratic humor layered over sincere social commentary about loneliness and disconnection. Names are deliberately overwrought and formal. The fictional framing satirizes real phenomena (corporate capture, government inefficiency, radical activism) while the underlying message — that human connection matters and is worth fighting for — is played completely straight. The comedy never undermines the sincerity; it's the delivery mechanism for it.

### MB3 Narrative Arc

For the third edition, the central storyline involves Carrefoogle Corporation sponsoring the festival itself — raising questions about corporate co-optation of authentic community spaces. This is being developed through a writers room process.

## What the Festival Actually Feels Like

Imagine arriving at a rural site near Barcelona. You take a "Separatonin vaccine" (an orange juice shot) at the entrance. You're greeted by actors in character as Bureau officials processing your intake. Over the next three days, professional chefs cook you beautiful meals, DJs and musicians play through the nights, workshops run during the day, theatrical skits and Syndicate "interventions" happen unexpectedly, and the whole thing builds toward some kind of narrative climax. There's no money changing hands inside. Everyone is there because they want to be part of making something together. You leave sleep-deprived, well-fed, and genuinely moved.

## Themes to Keep in Mind

- Social atomization and loneliness as the core concern (Putnam, Oldenburg, Fisher, Murthy)
- Gift economy and non-commodified space as praxis, not just ideology
- Satire as a vehicle for sincerity — never cynical
- The tension between institutional approaches (Bureau) and radical direct action (Syndicate) to social problems
- Corporate co-optation of authentic community (Carrefoogle)
- DIY culture, rave culture, and the radical potential of collective joy
- The festival as a "temporary autonomous zone" or liminal space where different social rules apply
