History of the Playground Bureau

1. Background
The year was 1980. Catherine “Cat” Sandwich, a completely unknown candidate with a 50€ campaign budget, was elected head of state in a record-breaking 99%-to-1% landslide victory by capturing the hearts and minds of the country with a simple slogan: “How’s your neighbor doing?” While other candidates debated stock prices and GDP forecasts, Cat Sandwich argued that a country’s total wealth meant nothing if its people were not having a good time on this beautiful little planet.
Inspired by Bhutan’s Gross National Happiness concept, Cat Sandwich recalibrated the government’s goal towards maximizing Citizen Goodtimes, which meant not only ensuring fundamental material needs, but asking a question no government had bothered with: are people actually having a good time?
Soon her team of scientists discovered Goodtimesium (GTM), a quantum-neuro-molecular-something-or-other emitted by human beings in states of genuine connection, joy, and collective silliness. High GTM readings became the government’s north star.
2. The Bureau’s Origin
As countries around the globe followed Cat Sandwich’s lead, a certain Playground Bureau—a municipal parks office tasked with designing and maintaining playgrounds—drew attention. It was discovered that playgrounds designed by the Bureau produced unusually high Goodtimesium readings when kids were playing in them. Gradually, then suddenly, the Playground Bureau was elevated from a humble parks office to a full government agency, tasked with GTM-elevation strategies for the entire country.
They insisted on keeping the name Playground Bureau as a homage to their humble beginnings.
3. The Separatoxin Threat
In the early 2000s, Bureau scientists detected alarming readings of a counter-phenomenon: Separatoxin, a mysterious force linked to declining social connection across populations. Its precise nature remains under investigation. The Bureau acknowledged the threat but maintained its focus: the best defence against Separatoxin was to keep elevating Goodtimesium levels. Not everyone agreed.
4. The Syndicate
Not everyone believed the Bureau was moving fast enough. A renegade splinter group, rumored to include disgruntled ex-Bureau agents, began operating outside official channels under the name The Syndicate.
Exhibit 1: Insignia recovered from confiscated propaganda materials
They accused the Bureau of bureaucratic complacency and excessive tolerance of corporate interests. Their methods were direct: defacing corporate installations, hijacking sound equipment for unsanctioned parties, and distributing propaganda in tinfoil hats (to insulate from “7G”). The Bureau has officially classified the Syndicate as a nuisance while the Syndicate has officially classified the Bureau as a sellout.
