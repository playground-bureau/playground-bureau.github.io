The year was 1980. Catherine "Cat" Sandwich, a completely known candidate with a 50€ campaign budget, was elected head of state in a record-breaking 99%-to-1% landslide victory, capturing the hearts and minds of the country with a simple slogan: "How's your neighbor doing?" While other candidates (including a washed-up movie star) debated stock prices and GDP forecasts, Cat Sandwich argued that a country's total wealth meant nothing if its people were not having Goodtimes on this beautiful little planet.

Taking from Bhutan's Gross National Happiness (link: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gross_National_Happiness) concept, Cat Sandwich recalibrated the government's goal towards maximizing Citizen Goodtimes, which meant not only ensuring fundamental material needs, but asking a question no government had bothered with: are people actually having a good time?

Soon her team of scientists discovered Goodtimesium (GTM), a quantum/neuro/molecular/whatever thing that is emitted by human beings in states of genuine connection, joy, and collective silliness. Despite some concerns that this discovery may lead to yet another metric-chacing, the measurement methodologies and quotas were enshrined into the govenment playbook. Proponents argued that while it is not perfect, it is far more preferable to chacing GDP.

As countries around the globe followed Cat Sandwich's lead, a certain <strong>Playground Bureau</strong>, a municipal parks office tasked with designing and maintaining playgrounds, caught attention. It was discovered that playgrounds designed by the Bureau had an unusually high readings of Goodtimesium when kids were playing in it. Slowly, their endeavors were recognized, and gradually, then suddenly, Playground Bureau was tasked with GMT-maximization methods for all of the country.
